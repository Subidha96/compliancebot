VERBATIM SUMMARY — G20/OECD Principles of Corporate Governance 2023
Source: Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD)
Official: https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/g20-oecd-principles-of-corporate-governance-2023_ed750b30-en.html
================================================================================

The G20/OECD Principles of Corporate Governance (2023 revision) are the
global standard for corporate governance policy. They are intended to help
policy makers evaluate and improve the legal, regulatory, and institutional
framework for corporate governance. First issued in 1999, last revised in
2023, and endorsed by G20 Leaders.

I. ENSURING THE BASIS FOR AN EFFECTIVE CORPORATE GOVERNANCE FRAMEWORK

   The corporate governance framework should promote transparent and efficient
   markets, and the efficient allocation of resources. It should be consistent
   with the rule of law and clearly articulate the division of responsibilities
   among different authorities.

   A. The corporate governance framework should be developed with a view to
      its impact on corporate access to finance, overall economic performance
      and financial stability, the sustainability and resilience of corporations.
   B. Legal and regulatory requirements should be consistent with the rule of
      law, transparent and enforceable. Corporate governance codes may offer
      a complementary mechanism to support best practices.
   C. The division of responsibilities among different authorities should be
      clearly articulated and ensure that the public interest is served.
   D. Stock market regulation should support effective corporate governance.
   E. Supervisory, regulatory and enforcement authorities should have the
      authority, autonomy, integrity and resources to fulfil their duties in
      a professional and objective manner.
   F. Digital technologies can enhance supervision and implementation of
      corporate governance requirements, but authorities should manage
      associated risks.
   G. Cross-border co-operation should be enhanced through bilateral and
      multilateral arrangements for exchange of information.
   H. Clear regulatory frameworks should ensure effective oversight of
      publicly traded companies within company groups.

II. THE RIGHTS AND EQUITABLE TREATMENT OF SHAREHOLDERS AND KEY OWNERSHIP FUNCTIONS

   The corporate governance framework should ensure equitable treatment of all
   shareholders, including minority and foreign shareholders. All shareholders
   should have the opportunity to obtain effective redress for violation of
   their rights.

   Key provisions:
   - Shareholders should have the right to participate in decisions concerning
     changes to the corporate governance framework (e.g., amendments to the
     articles of association).
   - Shareholders should have the right to receive relevant and timely
     information about the company, including financial performance, governance
     structure, and major shareholdings.
   - The share transfer process should be transparent and efficient.
   - Controlling shareholders should not exercise their power to the detriment
     of minority shareholders.
   - Related-party transactions should be approved at arm's length and with
     proper disclosure.
   - Shareholders should have the opportunity to ask questions at the AGM,
     including questions about financial statements and governance practices.

III. INSTITUTIONAL INVESTORS, STOCK MARKETS, AND OTHER INTERMEDIARIES

   Institutional investors acting in a fiduciary capacity should disclose their
   overall corporate governance and voting policies with respect to their
   investments.

   Key provisions:
   - Institutional investors should disclose how they manage material conflicts
     of interest that may affect the exercise of key ownership rights.
   - Institutional investors should be allowed to consult with each other on
     issues concerning basic shareholder rights.
   - Stock exchanges should ensure that listed companies comply with governance
     standards as a condition of listing.
   - Market intermediaries should act in the best interests of their clients.

IV. DISCLOSURE AND TRANSPARENCY

   The corporate governance framework should ensure that timely and accurate
   disclosure is made on all material matters regarding the corporation,
   including the financial situation, performance, ownership, and governance.

   Key provisions:
   - Annual and interim financial reports should be prepared in accordance
     with national or international accounting standards (IFRS).
   - Auditing standards should be of high quality and ensure independence
     of the external auditor.
   - Material information about the company's governance should be disclosed,
     including board composition, remuneration, related-party transactions,
     and risk management policies.
   - Information should be accessible to all users at the same time and
     disclosed in a timely manner.
   - A company's equity ownership structure should be disclosed, including
     controlling shareholders and cross-shareholdings.
   - Projections and forecasts should be disclosed where material to investment
     decisions.

V. THE RESPONSIBILITIES OF THE BOARD

   The board should act in the best interests of the company and its shareholders.
   The board should have appropriate size, composition, and structures to
   fulfil its responsibilities effectively.

   Key provisions:
   V.A. Board members should act on a fully informed basis, in good faith,
        with due diligence and care, in the best interest of the company
        and its shareholders.
   V.B. Where board decisions may affect different shareholder groups equally,
        the board should treat all shareholders fairly.
   V.C. The board should apply high ethical standards. It should take into
        account the interests of stakeholders and consider the broader
        impact of its decisions on society and the environment.
   V.D. Board structures and practices should be reviewed periodically and
        disclosed to shareholders.

   V.D.1. Board Size and Composition
          The board should have an appropriate size and composition. It should
          have a sufficient number of non-executive members, including
          independent directors, to exercise objective judgement.
          - Board size should be appropriate for the company's size and
            complexity.
          - The board should have a mix of skills, experience, diversity,
            and independence.
          - A sufficient proportion of non-executive directors should be
            independent of management and free from any material business
            or other relationship.

   V.D.2. Board Leadership
          The board should select a chairperson and, if necessary, a lead
          independent director. The roles of chairperson and CEO should
          be separated or, if combined, the board should justify the
          decision and have a strong lead independent director.

   V.D.3. Board Committees
          The board may establish committees to assist in carrying out its
          responsibilities. Common committees include:
          - Audit Committee: oversees financial reporting and disclosure,
            internal controls, and external audit.
          - Risk Management Committee: oversees the risk management framework.
          - Remuneration Committee: sets compensation policies for executives.
          - Nomination Committee: recommends candidates for board positions.
          Committees should have clear terms of reference and report to the
          full board.

   V.D.4. Selecting, Overseeing and Monitoring Performance of Key Executives
          The board should oversee the performance of key executives and
          monitor that their actions are consistent with the strategy and
          policies approved by the board. The board should select the CEO
          and may select other key executives.

VI. SUSTAINABILITY AND RESILIENCE

   The corporate governance framework should promote transparency and disclosure
   of sustainability-related matters. Companies should consider the interests
   of a broad range of stakeholders, including employees, customers, creditors,
   suppliers, local communities, and the environment.

   Key provisions:
   - The board should oversee the company's approach to environmental, social,
     and governance (ESG) risks and opportunities.
   - Companies should disclose material sustainability-related information
     in their annual reports.
   - The board should ensure that the company's strategy accounts for
     climate-related and other sustainability risks and opportunities.
   - Stakeholder engagement should inform the company's governance and
     decision-making processes.

GRC RELEVANCE FOR NEPAL:
   - NRB Directive No. 2 (Nepal's banking governance framework) is broadly
     aligned with OECD Principles, particularly on board composition,
     audit committees, risk management, and disclosure.
   - Nepal's Companies Act 2063 incorporates several OECD recommendations,
     including independent directors for public companies, mandatory audit
     committees, and shareholder protection mechanisms.
   - However, Nepal faces challenges in enforcement, independent director
     availability, and related-party transaction oversight that the OECD
     Principles seek to address.
